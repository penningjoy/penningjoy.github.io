There's a phrase Dieter Rams returned to for forty years: *Weniger, aber besser.* Less, but better. It is easy to nod at and hard to live by, because it does not ask you to do less work. It asks you to do the harder work of deciding what deserves to exist.

This site is the first place I tried to mean it.

## The constraint is the design

I gave myself two rules before writing a line. Greyscale only. And as little machinery as possible — no framework I didn't need, no font I had to download, no script that wasn't earning its place.

Both rules took things away. Both made the result better.

Without colour, hierarchy has to come from somewhere honest: the size of a word, the weight of a line, the air around a paragraph. You feel where to look because the **structure** tells you, not because something was painted to grab you.

> Good design is as little design as possible. Less, but better — because it concentrates on the essential aspects, and the products are not burdened with non-essentials.

## What it changes in practice

- **Speed becomes a feature you don't talk about.** The page is here before you notice it loading. That's principle nine — and it's also just respect.
- **Honesty becomes visible.** No fake depth, no borrowed credibility from stock photography. The materials are real: type, rule, space.
- **The details get the attention.** When there's less to make, you can afford to get the small things right — the tracking on a heading, the rhythm between sections, the way a link underlines.

## Why a product builder should care

I don't think of this as a designer's indulgence. I think it's how lovable products get made. The discipline of *less, but better* is the discipline of knowing what the thing is for — and having the nerve to remove everything that isn't that.

That's the work. Everything else is decoration.
